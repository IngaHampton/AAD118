For the micro section, I have to simply take photos that consist of close-up individual items or objects found within the area that were taken for the macro images.

The Lighting

Lighting is an important aspect to photography, as lighting can completely change the mood and look to a photograph. Photography usually takes place in daylight, best in the morning and evening around an hour and a half after sunrise and before sunset. If photography is done during the night, the use of ambient light, streetlights and moonlight can also create a great effect.

The Viewpoint.

A photographs mood can change all down to the alteration of the angle and viewpoint it has been taken. When an image has been captured and the camera is placed on the ground, it gives the image a whole different look, as if the image is much larger, or that it is looking over you. The opposite action can apply as well, looking down at an image, making the subject look smaller or to get its viewpoint.

The Rule of Thirds.

The rule of thirds is a widely known system used by photographers, as a guideline it can enhance and add appeal to your photographs. When this principle is used the image is equally divided onto nine segments (3x3 grid), two vertical lines and two horizontal, this creates a grid that helps keep the photograph proportionally correct as the cross-section between the lines keeps the picture aligned. Most cameras, even phone cameras include this grid feature.

Once you know the rules, break them.

“There are no rules for good photographs, there are only good photographs.”
– Ansel Adams

From blurry images to strange angles, rules are there to be broken. Once we had learnt all about the guidelines that are out there for beginner photographers, we were allowed to ignore some of the most well known rules out there, such as the Rule of Thirds. It's all about experimenting!

The History

The earliest known surviving photograph, ‘View from the Window at le Gras’ dated way back in 1826. It was captured by Joseph Niépce the French inventor and pioneer of photography. The photograph was taken on a photoengraved printing plate using a camera obscura.

A Creative or art Director is a given role that is usually within in the design, entertainment and fashion industries. An art director is a position that is considered the top of the company when it comes to design. They have a great impact on the work that is produced. To be at this level it can take many years, many years of experience within the design industry is needed to achieve this position. The career is known to be a very challenging and stressful job, due to the extraordinary attention to detail that is essential. The purpose of the role is to lead teams of creative experts by working with them in order to produce excellent results for their clients.

Art Directors need to be able to communicate well within a group or team, so they would need to have exceptional presentation and communication skills, with the capability to listen, respond and relate to clients - in particular to explain things clearly to people who may not be very accustomed with multimedia and design products and solutions. Josef Müller-Brockmann

Josef Müller-Brockmann is by far one of my favourite graphic designers, as I love his iconic block colour and use of typography that is seen within Swiss international style. The Swiss graphic designer and teacher was born in Rapperswil in 1914. Having studied architecture and history of art at the University of Kunstgewerbeschule in Zurich, he began his career by specialising in both photography and of graphic design. He was heavily influenced by art movements such as De Stijl and the Bauhaus which helped him create and develop his own style.

Several books were published by Josef Müller-Brockmann, such books including The Graphic Artist and His Problems, and Grid Systems in Graphic Design in 1961 where he explains his love for the grid system and how he uses it. Grid structures are heavily used with his work, this suggests that his architectural background was an influence, as ensuring everything was equal and correct was the upmost importance. Josef Muller-Brockmann is probably most well known for his use of simple designs that have the impression that they are random, but they are very accurately placed within a grid system. He used typography in such a clean and sophisticated way that it appeared so striking and effective that many designers still adapt his style today.

“ The grid system is an aid, not a guarantee. It permits a number of possible uses and each designer can look for a solution appropriate to his personal style. But one must learn how to use the grid; it is an art that requires practice. ”
- Josef Müller-Brockmann

Designing and deceiving with Data

This week we looked at Edward Tufte. We owe Edward Tufte a lot as he was one of the first founders of data visualisation. A complete expert when it comes to presenting information in an effective graphical way. His membership with the American statistical Association demonstrates his exceptional skills when it comes to creating charts, graphs and diagrams to precision. He is a man that is obsessed with perfection as well as uniqueness, he takes it to extreme and deletes almost every photograph that he takes as well as a lot of his written work. He works well of others feedback and from constructive criticism which allows him to step back and have a lot at what he could improve on.

Edward Tufte has spent so much time and effort working on visual literacy that he has even created some of his own terms for issues that he regularly comes across whilst critiquing design. The term ‘chartjunk’ was born when he came across so many useless and non-informative sections in information displays.

Edward Tufte has always been critical about Microsoft PowerPoint and his point was proved after the NASA Columbia Disaster. PowerPoints purpose was to be used as a way of information an audience about things and perhaps persuading them to think a certain way. PowerPoint should not have been used as a method of showing important technical details. Edward Tufte did an analysis on the NASA slide that was in question. This particular slide was overly crowd with information and included the important detail as small as possible as well as blurred. If this information had of been displayed correctly and effectively then perhaps lives could have been saved since the anomaly may have been noticed.

“Information designer Edward Tufte has claimed that the Challenger accident is an example of the problems that can occur from the lack of clarity in the presentation of information. He argues that if Morton Thiokol engineers had more clearly presented the data that they had on the relationship between low temperatures and burn-through in the solid rocket booster joints, they might have succeeded in persuading NASA managers to cancel the launch. To demonstrate this, he took all of the data he claimed the engineers had presented during the briefing, and reformatted it onto a single graph of O-ring damage versus external launch temperature, showing the effects of cold on the degree of O-ring damage. Tufte then placed the proposed launch of Challenger on the graph according to its predicted temperature at launch. According to Tufte, the launch temperature of Challenger was so far beyond from the coldest launch with the worst damage ever seen to date, that even a casual observer could have determined that the risk of disaster was severe.” -wikipedia
